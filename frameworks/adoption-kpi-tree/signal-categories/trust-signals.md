# Trust Signals

Trust signals show whether people believe a system, workflow or capability can be used safely, usefully and appropriately.

Trust is especially important when adoption depends on judgment, accountability, sensitive information, human review or AI-supported work.

## Core principle

Trust is not the same as satisfaction.

People may like a tool but not trust it for important work.

They may trust governance but not find the workflow useful.

They may use a system frequently while still feeling uncertain, pressured or exposed.

```text id="tbmn46"
Trust signals show whether people are willing to use the new capability when it matters.
```

## What trust signals can show

Trust signals can indicate:

* psychological safety
* perceived clarity
* perceived usefulness
* perceived legitimacy
* confidence
* adoption readiness
* hidden resistance
* fear of misuse or blame
* trust in review standards
* trust in governance
* trust in escalation paths
* trust in leadership intent
* willingness to use the system for meaningful tasks

## What trust signals cannot prove alone

Trust signals do not automatically prove:

* quality
* value
* proficiency
* workflow integration
* responsible use
* readiness for scaling

Trust must be interpreted together with behavior, quality, capability, governance and risk signals.

## Common trust signals

Examples include:

* user confidence in applying the workflow
* perceived clarity of rules
* perceived usefulness
* perceived safety to ask questions
* perceived fairness of accountability
* confidence in review standards
* confidence in escalation paths
* manager confidence in team readiness
* reviewer confidence in output quality
* employee willingness to use the system for meaningful tasks
* willingness to escalate uncertainty
* willingness to use approved workflows instead of private workarounds
* trust in how data or inputs are handled
* trust in leadership’s reason for adoption
* trust that mistakes or questions will not be punished
* trust that human judgment remains valued

## Weak vs. strong trust signals

Weak trust signal:

```text id="r3u2z3"
Users say they like the tool.
```

Stronger trust signal:

```text id="eonh8k"
Users are willing to use the workflow for meaningful tasks and escalate uncertainty when needed.
```

Weak trust signal:

```text id="rlh4cv"
Employees feel confident.
```

Stronger trust signal:

```text id="ir7yha"
Employees can explain what they are accountable for and where review is required.
```

Weak trust signal:

```text id="a0gq5q"
Managers support the initiative.
```

Stronger trust signal:

```text id="fnrxqs"
Managers discuss adoption openly, including friction, mistakes and uncertainty.
```

## Trust signal maturity

### Level 1: Awareness trust

```text id="eveh66"
People understand why the change exists.
```

### Level 2: Usefulness trust

```text id="7156c2"
People believe the capability can help with relevant work.
```

### Level 3: Safety trust

```text id="8ongr2"
People feel safe to try, ask questions and escalate uncertainty.
```

### Level 4: Accountability trust

```text id="ge9ogh"
People understand what they are responsible for and what remains reviewed or governed.
```

### Level 5: Workflow trust

```text id="9m9j8w"
People trust the workflow enough to use it in meaningful tasks.
```

### Level 6: Scaling trust

```text id="vkc4i4"
People trust the system enough for broader, repeatable adoption.
```

## Related signal categories

Trust signals should usually be interpreted together with:

* usage signals
* activation signals
* workflow signals
* capability signals
* risk signals
* governance signals
* quality signals

Trust explains why people may use, avoid, misuse or work around a system.

## Interpretation patterns

### Usage is high but trust is low

This may indicate:

* adoption is driven by pressure
* people use the system but feel exposed
* accountability is unclear
* governance is not trusted
* review standards are unclear
* leadership intent is questioned
* people fear mistakes or blame
* use is performative rather than meaningful

### Trust is high but usage is low

This may indicate:

* workflow relevance is weak
* access is difficult
* timing is poor
* effort is too high
* the first step is unclear
* the tool is not needed often
* use cases are not concrete enough

### Trust in tool is high but trust in governance is low

This may indicate:

* people like the capability but do not understand rules
* accountability feels unfair
* escalation paths are unclear
* review responsibilities are not trusted
* risk handling feels punitive

### Trust in governance is high but value is low

This may indicate:

* the organization has safe guardrails but weak use cases
* the workflow is too slow or heavy
* the system feels responsible but not useful
* adoption is safe but not compelling

## Decision logic examples

### High usage and low trust

```text id="7qyrss"
If usage is high but trust is low,
then adoption may be driven by pressure rather than conviction.

Action:
Investigate role clarity, accountability concerns and perceived risk.
```

### High trust and low usage

```text id="zhg51g"
If trust is high but usage is low,
then the issue may be workflow relevance, effort or access rather than resistance.

Action:
Improve integration into actual tasks.
```

### Low trust in escalation

```text id="xluoqx"
If people avoid escalation despite uncertainty,
then the escalation path may not feel safe or useful.

Action:
Clarify escalation purpose, response expectations and non-punitive learning logic.
```

### Confidence without capability

```text id="p4dy4z"
If confidence is high but review issues increase,
then users may be overconfident.

Action:
Add examples, quality criteria and feedback loops.
```

### Trust declines during rollout

```text id="fz0jv3"
If trust declines during rollout,
then adoption may be creating uncertainty about accountability, workload or intent.

Action:
Review communication, manager routines and decision rights.
```

## Countermetrics

Trust metrics should be paired with countermetrics.

| Trust metric                | Countermetric                |
| --------------------------- | ---------------------------- |
| Higher confidence           | Quality after review         |
| Higher perceived usefulness | Actual workflow use          |
| Higher perceived safety     | Escalation behavior          |
| Fewer concerns raised       | Hidden underreporting        |
| More willingness to use     | Risk understanding           |
| Higher trust in rules       | Correct application of rules |
| Higher manager confidence   | Team capability              |
| Higher trust in output      | Review pass rate             |

## Questions for adoption reviews

Use these questions when reviewing trust signals:

```text id="r380mu"
Do people trust the system for meaningful work?
Do they understand what they are accountable for?
Do they feel safe to ask questions?
Do they escalate uncertainty early?
Do they trust review and governance processes?
Do they understand why the change is happening?
Is usage driven by usefulness or pressure?
Where is trust low by role, workflow or risk level?
Is confidence supported by quality and capability?
What would make the workflow feel safer or more useful?
```

## Common mistakes

Avoid these mistakes:

* treating satisfaction as trust
* treating high usage as trust
* ignoring fear of blame
* ignoring accountability concerns
* measuring trust without role segmentation
* assuming trust is only emotional
* ignoring whether trust is supported by quality
* failing to distinguish trust in tool, workflow, governance and leadership

## Final principle

Trust signals show whether adoption is psychologically and operationally credible.

```text id="pks4yr"
People adopt more sustainably when they understand the value, the rules, the limits and their own role in the system.
```
