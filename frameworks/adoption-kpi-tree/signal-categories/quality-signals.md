# Quality Signals

Quality signals show whether outputs, decisions or processes improve or remain reliable as adoption increases.

They are essential when adoption affects communication, knowledge work, service quality, decision support, regulated workflows or AI-supported outputs.

## Core principle

Adoption should not be judged by speed or usage alone.

It must also be judged by whether the work remains good enough, reliable enough and reviewable enough.

```text id="y889hd"
Adoption without quality signals can turn speed into hidden rework.
```

## What quality signals can show

Quality signals can indicate:

* proficiency
* reliability
* output fit
* process stability
* review effectiveness
* quality gate maturity
* readiness for scaling
* whether speed gains are real
* whether work is shifting downstream
* whether standards are understood and applied
* whether inputs are good enough to support better outputs

## What quality signals cannot prove alone

Quality signals do not automatically prove:

* user trust
* value creation
* broad adoption
* workflow integration
* governance maturity
* scalability

Quality should be interpreted together with usage, workflow, value, risk and capability signals.

## Common quality signals

Examples include:

* correction rate after review
* review rejection rate
* number of avoidable review loops
* completeness of input briefs
* consistency with standards
* clarity of sources or references
* audience fit
* use-case fit
* fewer missing requirements
* fewer unclear responsibilities
* better alignment with quality criteria
* improved reviewer feedback themes
* fewer downstream corrections
* fewer unsupported claims
* fewer formatting or structural corrections
* fewer repeated error types
* stronger handover quality
* clearer distinction between draft, reviewed and approved output

## Weak vs. strong quality signals

Weak quality signal:

```text id="u8mop9"
Users say the output is good.
```

Stronger quality signal:

```text id="y5o46i"
Reviewed outputs meet defined quality criteria with fewer avoidable corrections.
```

Weak quality signal:

```text id="s8krlu"
The workflow is faster.
```

Stronger quality signal:

```text id="vjo31w"
The workflow is faster without increasing downstream correction or review effort.
```

Weak quality signal:

```text id="wgznl6"
Fewer complaints.
```

Stronger quality signal:

```text id="wl6m1n"
Recurring review issues decrease across comparable workflow cycles.
```

## Quality signal maturity

### Level 1: Output exists

```text id="mxugfe"
The new workflow produces outputs.
```

### Level 2: Output reviewed

```text id="c420qz"
Outputs pass through the intended review path.
```

### Level 3: Output meets minimum standard

```text id="mu3whe"
Outputs meet basic quality criteria.
```

### Level 4: Review effort decreases

```text id="7hiqyb"
Avoidable corrections and repeated issues decrease.
```

### Level 5: Quality stabilizes

```text id="tiowyd"
Quality remains reliable across users, teams or workflow cycles.
```

### Level 6: Quality improves with adoption

```text id="jgcc6n"
Adoption produces better consistency, clearer inputs or stronger output fit.
```

## Related signal categories

Quality signals should usually be interpreted together with:

* workflow signals
* capability signals
* value signals
* risk signals
* governance signals
* scaling signals

Quality is especially important before scaling.

## Interpretation patterns

### Usage increases and quality improves

This may indicate:

* workflow fit is strong
* users are becoming proficient
* examples and templates are working
* review criteria are clear
* the use case may be ready for expansion

### Usage increases and quality declines

This may indicate:

* adoption volume is outpacing proficiency
* guidance is insufficient
* users are applying the capability too broadly
* review gates are weak
* inputs are poor
* scaling is premature

### Cycle time improves and correction rate increases

This may indicate:

* speed gains are not real
* work is shifting downstream
* pre-review quality is weak
* handover standards are unclear
* reviewers are absorbing the cost of adoption

### Quality is stable but value is limited

This may indicate:

* adoption is safe but shallow
* the use case is too narrow
* teams are using the capability conservatively
* stronger value requires workflow redesign
* scaling may need different use cases

## Decision logic examples

### Faster work with more corrections

```text id="nu58re"
If cycle time improves but correction rate increases,
then speed may be shifting work downstream.

Action:
Improve pre-review quality gates before expanding adoption.
```

### Repeated quality issue

```text id="avze2w"
If the same quality issue appears repeatedly,
then the issue is probably systemic rather than individual.

Action:
Update the input brief, checklist, standard or example library.
```

### High usage with low quality

```text id="x1c7dd"
If usage is high but review rejection is high,
then adoption volume may be outpacing proficiency.

Action:
Slow scaling and improve examples, input quality and review criteria.
```

### Quality stable only with expert support

```text id="vse82q"
If quality is stable only when experts provide heavy support,
then adoption may not yet be scalable.

Action:
Translate expert judgment into examples, checklists and decision rules.
```

### Quality improves but adoption remains narrow

```text id="v39tt8"
If quality improves but adoption remains limited to a small group,
then the pattern may be strong but not yet transferable.

Action:
Identify what enables quality and turn it into reusable guidance.
```

## Countermetrics

Quality metrics should be paired with countermetrics.

| Quality metric          | Countermetric            |
| ----------------------- | ------------------------ |
| Lower correction rate   | Slower cycle time        |
| Faster approval         | Missed review issues     |
| Fewer review loops      | Lower contextual fit     |
| Higher consistency      | Generic outputs          |
| Fewer missing inputs    | More upfront effort      |
| Higher review pass rate | Hidden reviewer leniency |
| Stable quality          | Limited adoption depth   |
| Reduced expert support  | Increased risk or errors |

## Questions for adoption reviews

Use these questions when reviewing quality signals:

```text id="os6k1t"
Is quality improving, stable or declining?
Compared to what baseline?
Where do quality issues appear?
Are issues individual or systemic?
Are review criteria clear?
Are inputs good enough?
Are speed gains creating downstream work?
Which quality issues repeat?
Which quality gates are missing or weak?
Is quality stable enough to scale?
```

## Common mistakes

Avoid these mistakes:

* measuring speed without review effort
* measuring usage without output quality
* blaming individuals for systemic quality issues
* scaling before quality is stable
* ignoring repeated reviewer feedback
* treating stable quality as sufficient without checking value
* failing to define quality criteria
* using vague quality language without observable indicators

## Final principle

Quality signals protect adoption from false success.

```text id="om8e27"
Adoption is not mature because more work is produced.
Adoption is mature when better work becomes easier to produce reliably.
```
